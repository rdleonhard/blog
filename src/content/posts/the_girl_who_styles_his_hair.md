I skipped a step from last post.

Last post cautioned that you should become soft to avoid getting swatted. It's an analogy in the form of a narrative. A "fable" if you will.

But I didn't mention our secret: how we know who's a bad guy and who's a good guy. Nobody mentions it, maybe nobody put it together yet. Maybe you can't find it in history because it's in her story.

How do you know someone's dangerous? Somebody has to narc on him.

It's not going to be the chief, who sees him around the campfire on his best behavior, same as everyone else. The secret monster is a perfect gentleman in the common areas. That's the trick. The threat never shows up when the powerful are looking.

It strikes in the dark. In the tent. To the weak.

The one who knows what kind of man he actually is. The one who sleeps next to him. She knows him better than he knows himself. But she's not allowed to speak. She can't risk angering him. And who would care anyway?

My observation is that she can communicate to others in society that he's dangerous. And she does so with such subtlety that he doesn't even realize it. Indeed, if this is your first time thinking of it, you never realized until today.

She grooms him.

This is a habit formed out of necessity, inherited from our forest-dwelling ancestors. A method of removing ticks, and other parasites. Perhaps even the goosebumps we feel when fingers run down our necks are used to spread the hair out to give the groomer a better view of the landscape.

And so a well groomed man signals biological health. But it also signals something else, I'd argue: an archetype.

That dangerous man you imagined has a particular style about him. The pointy beard reads as devilish. Clean, kept hair that's rounded reads as safe. And all the different styles in between send a message, a signal: "This is the type of guy who..."

Is dumb, but loyal? Is big and nice, but lazy? Is small but vicious?

The groomers, or let's use the word "stylist" since the former has some baggage that's irrelevant to the point I'm trying to make, are saying something about the man. About his character. His temperament. His story. The stylist is an artist and the man is her art piece. She practices on herself and her friends, but for them it's not to send signals, but noise. She covers blemishes. She uses red clay or blood to redden her cheeks. She's telling you lies, can you hear them?

But for the man, it's all signal. She's not punishing him or rewarding him. She's telling the rest of the flock: "this guy's fine," or "watch out for this one."

She knows him intimately. And she gets to tell his story through his hair. It works because it's the one signal he can't fake. In many cases, he's completely blind to style. It's the language of the powerless, the oppressed. He can charm by the fireside all night, but it won't matter. His hair is telling a different story.

Maybe he's on to her. Maybe he starts to ask about her art. He then finds himself in the archetype of sorcerer: "Stay away, he's evil. Don't listen to him, he'll trick you."

And that's what this is. You don't testify before a court. You don't call the police. You shape his very identity.

It's not her only trick. There are many. Don't even try to learn. You will find yourself as someone else entirely.

She doesn't do it intentionally. She's merely sculpting what she already sees. All you can do it behave. It's her panopticon, and you're just a guest here.
