The infiltrator is a clown.

He shows up one day biting your style, using your slang, suddenly furious about the exact thing you're furious about. *How do you do, fellow kids.* And you're too polite to call it. Or maybe you clocked him as a fed on day one and waved him in anyway, out of sheer disbelief that anyone cares enough about your little group to bother planting somebody in it.

Either way, it works. It's worked for centuries, across culture after culture, on institutions that should know better by now. And it works because he isn't doing anything we don't do. He's wearing colors. We just happen to recognize them as ours.

I was in Iraq during Saddam's trial. He grew out a beard, found religion on camera, did the whole devout-elder routine, and it was laughable, because he'd spent decades as the same clean-shaven strongman with the dictator mustache, and now all of a sudden he's a humble cleric. A costume change. The colors said *save me, brothers,* and nobody bought it. You can't throw on colors you've never worn and expect the room not to notice the tag still hanging off them.

Now go back a long way. To the apostle Paul. If that is his real name.

By his own account, and this is him bragging in his own letters, to the Greeks he's a Greek, all Stoic logic. To the Jews he's a Jew, Torah and messianic signs. To the Romans he's selling something that might just hold the empire together. That's past code-switching. The man's a chameleon, and a chameleon has no color of its own. My read: he was a Roman plant. His entire prior career was rounding up early Christians for arrest, and I don't buy that a guy flips that hard off a single flash of light on a road. Notice, too, that the second he and Peter have it out, the story quietly stops being about Peter. The whole book becomes the Paul show.

*You can't prove that!*

I know. And here's the thing. I shouldn't have to. The man's profession was betrayal. When the one line on the résumé is *infiltrate and inform,* you're allowed to never trust a word he says again. The burden isn't on me to prove the chameleon is faking a color. It's on the color to prove it's real.

It can go the other way, though. The asset goes native. Every handler's worst nightmare. You do the Avatar thing, fall for the local blue-skin savage woman, switch sides for real. So how do you stop that?

An authoritarian regime has an easy answer: terror. Hostage families, defectors shot in the street, the whole apparatus built to make leaving unthinkable. The rabid dog on a short leash, and the leash is punishment.

But what does a liberal democracy do? Here's where it gets interesting, because the lawyer in me hears that question as *what can it do?* We have to obey the law. So what's the actual ceiling on what you're allowed to do to a man who betrays you?

Say the honest answer is "nothing, short of a jury and a prison cell." Fine. Let me push on it. Can I talk shit about him online? Obviously. The First Amendment's got me, at least in my personal capacity. Can I scare the hell out of him? *That's* the real question, and it's exactly the strip of ground these countries live on. What if you never laid a finger on anyone, but everyone *believed* you had? You'd get the reputation for free. You'd put the fear of God into every would-be defector without ever committing the act.

Is that legal? You couldn't even research it properly without classified court orders and statutory readings nobody outside the building is cleared to read. But I'll say it: I think it probably is. What's the rule going to be, that the government is hereby forbidden from implying it does things it doesn't do? Can it quietly slip Alex Jones a stack of documents that make it look like there are men in vans for anyone who talks? Yeah. I think it can. There's a word for it. Theater. The asset never knows whether the threat is real, and the not-knowing does all the work. You don't have to hurt a single person.

Which is where the whole thing finally shows its shape. Strip it down and you get a clean moral question: if you had the choice, would you rather *be* evil, or just *pretend* to be?

Those are not the same product, and America runs hard on the second one. We pretend. We pretty much have to, because the rivals across the table aren't pretending. Putin doesn't perform menace. Neither does Xi. They run the opposite con. They perform *innocence.* They lie toward good, because the evil is already handled. We lie the other way. And it's the more forgivable lie, and it's a little funny, because there is something farcical about the most powerful country on earth doing cosplay villainy at home. Dressing up scary. Hinting it might be unhinged. Mostly to get one guy to stop leaking.

I couldn't put that down until I started thinking about bugs.

A poisonous insect wears bright colors on purpose. *I'm right here. Look all you want. I don't care, because eating me will kill you.* Honest signal. The colors are earned. There is real poison behind them. But then some harmless little nothing of a bug evolves the same bright colors and skips the poison entirely. *Why go to the trouble of being lethal,* it figures, *when I can just dress like I am?* Cheap warning, no product behind it. And it works, right up until something gets hungry enough to call the bluff.

At home, that's us. The harmless bug in borrowed colors. We can't legally be the real poison, can't run the torture state, so we grow the warning colors instead and bet nobody bites to check. It's Saddam's late beard. It's the passionate opening of Paul's epistles, working so hard to convince you he's sincere. It's the sweet kid who gets picked on one too many times and turns up in Hot Topic spikes and black lipstick, not because she's dangerous but because looking dangerous is the only armor she can afford. Forgivable. A little funny. Mostly harmless.

It's also the exact inverse of the move in my soft-boi post. There, the play was to be *genuinely* soft, to hide your teeth by filing them down, because faking softness gets you killed worse than just being hard. This is the same gap run the other way: no teeth at all, and a big toothy snarl painted over the gums.

But that's the home game. The cosplay only works because of what happens when somebody flies out to test it.

Osama had that famous line about America being a paper tiger, and he didn't mean it as hyperbole. He'd watched the Marines leave Beirut in '83 after one truck bomb and the Army leave Somalia in '93 after one bad night, and he decided the whole snarl was paint. The colors weren't earned. Bite, and nothing bites back. He made the one mistake the bug story warns you about. He saw a country *choosing* not to bite and called it a country that *couldn't.* He read "won't" as "can't."

So he bit. As hard as anyone ever has.

And he got his answer in a bedroom in Abbottabad. That's the trap, and it's the entire product: from the outside you cannot tell paint from poison. The not-knowing is the deterrent. The man who bet hardest in living memory that it was a bluff is the proof that it wasn't.

Maybe that's why Afganistan was 20 of meandering: to prove a point.

It's all theater until it's not. Or maybe that's just my worldview. No joke, I majored in theater in undergrad, right before I deployed to Iraq. Al-Qaeda did this thing where they'd hit the far superior US units first, get their asses handed to them, then decide to only go after the weaker-looking ones. So the whole game became not *looking* weak. How? Theater, my guy. You want your convoy looking like it's run by capable, gung-ho lunatics. Put on your war face and swivel that Mark 19 around. Show them you're ready for a fight.

My point is that I don't think we're trying to signal like we're Mr. Rogers, or Jimmy Carter on the world stage, anymore. That posture, ironically, led to more war. I think the signaling is more like that of the poisonous bug, and its up to the hungry whether they want to risk it.
